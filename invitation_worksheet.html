Reaching out to your spouse
A private worksheet to help you start the conversation

Before you send a message or pick up the phone, this worksheet helps you understand what your spouse may be feeling — and how to reach them in a way that opens a door rather than closing one.

Your answers stay with you. Nothing here is sent to anyone.

Part One

What might your spouse be feeling?

Select everything that sounds like your spouse right now. You can choose as many as feel true — most people show more than one.

When you imagine bringing up mediation, what is your spouse most likely to say?
What do you think your spouse is most afraid of?
When your spouse has pushed back on difficult conversations in the past, what did it look like?
What does your spouse need most right now to feel safe enough to take one small step?

Part Two

Before you reach out

These questions are for you, not for the message. Answer honestly. The clearer you are here, the better your outreach will land.

Not what you want them to feel. What they actually feel.
This doesn't mean taking blame. It means showing that you see them.
Heard? Respected? Not ambushed? Not accused?
This is the hardest question. It's also the most important one.

The C.H.I.L.D. communication framework
C
Child-FocusedKeep the children's wellbeing at the center, even in how you write
H
HonestSay what is true without embellishing or omitting what matters
I
IntentionalEvery word earns its place — nothing is vented, nothing is accidental
L
Low-HeatCalm in tone even when the situation isn't — de-escalate, don't trigger
D
DirectClear and firm about what you are asking, without pressure or ultimatum
