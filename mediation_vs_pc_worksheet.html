Which path is right for us?
A reflective worksheet for families considering mediation or parenting coordination in California

This worksheet is most useful when completed individually, before any joint session. If you and your co-parent are filling it out separately, that is expected — you do not need to share your answers with each other before speaking with a professional.

Your answers stay with you. Nothing here is sent to anyone. At the end, you'll have the option to use your answers to draft an outreach to your attorney or your co-parent — with AI help.

Part One

Your situation

Let's start with what's actually driving the conflict right now.

What is the primary source of conflict between you and your co-parent right now?
Select everything that applies.
Is the primary issue about co-parenting specifically — or are there other unresolved matters from the divorce that are driving or complicating things?
Mediation can address a wide range of issues. Parenting coordination focuses specifically on the parenting plan and child-related decisions.
Part Two

Your existing agreement

What is already in place — and what isn't working?

Do you already have a co-parenting or custody agreement in place?
What parts of your agreement — or your co-parenting relationship — are working reasonably well?
What parts are not working? Where does the conflict most often show up?
Part Three

Legal representation

This shapes how any process gets started and who needs to be involved.

Are you represented by an attorney in your divorce or custody case?
Is your co-parent represented by an attorney?
When one or both parties are represented, the decision to pursue mediation or parenting coordination will typically involve counsel. Attorneys are often the primary contact with the court, and any agreement will be reviewed before filing. If you have an attorney, talk to them before scheduling anything.
Part Four

Where things stand between you

Understanding your co-parent's position helps shape how you approach the next conversation.

Have you discussed the possibility of mediation or parenting coordination with your co-parent?
What do you think is getting in the way for your co-parent — if anything?
Not what you wish they felt. What you actually observe.
Part Five

The question that often decides the path

This is the most important question in the worksheet. Take your time with it.

When you and your co-parent cannot agree on a decision about your child, what do you want to happen?
A note on parenting coordination: Choosing a PC does not mean giving up your voice. You participate in every decision. The PC's authority to decide only activates when both parties have genuinely tried and failed to agree — and any decision can be brought to the court for review.
Part Six

Your safety and wellbeing

Every professional process needs to be designed around your specific situation. This question helps make sure nothing important gets missed.

A question worth answering honestly
Are there any safety concerns, power dynamics, or patterns in your co-parenting relationship that a professional should be aware of before designing a process with you?
This includes — but is not limited to — domestic violence, restraining orders, substance use, mental health concerns, or anything that affects how safely you can be in the same room or process together. You do not have to have a court order for this to matter.
Part Seven

Anything else

Is there anything about your situation — history, context, concerns about the process — that a professional should know before your first conversation?
